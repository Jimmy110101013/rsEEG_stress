\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

% Overview paragraph: tell the reader what story this section tells.
Our results are organised as three pillars that together support a single
thesis: the balanced accuracy (BA) that EEG foundation models report on
resting-state stress classification is not model-driven, and the
cross-dataset fine-tuning (FT) behaviour that does emerge is driven by a
model~$\times$~dataset interaction rather than by any one model's quality.
Pillar~A (\S\ref{sec:results:methodology}) establishes the three
methodology traps that inflate reported numbers; Pillar~B
(\S\ref{sec:results:representation}) diagnoses what the frozen
representations actually encode; Pillar~C
(\S\ref{sec:results:taxonomy}) is the headline cross-dataset taxonomy;
\S\ref{sec:results:interp} adds a TNSRE-flavoured neuroscience
interpretability panel; \S\ref{sec:power_floor} closes with the
statistical-power-floor argument that reframes every Stress-specific
claim in the paper as a \emph{benchmark-design caveat} rather than an
FM failure.

% ============================================================
\subsection{Pillar A: Methodology Traps}
\label{sec:results:methodology}

% Overview of Pillar A
Before any claim about an FM's stress-decoding ability can be trusted,
three methodological traps must be defused. Each of them has produced a
widely-cited number in the recent EEG literature; each of them reverses
or halves that number once defused. Figures~\ref{fig:cvgap}
and~\ref{fig:classical} and the cuDNN swing audit below make this
quantitative.

\subsubsection{Subject leakage inflates BA (F01)}

% MOTIVATION / EVIDENCE
The most common trial-level \textsc{Stratified\-KFold} evaluation on
UCSD Stress places recordings from the same subject on both sides of
the split, which the classifier can exploit via subject identity
instead of state. Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025stress} report 0.90 BA on
LaBraM under this protocol. Under our subject-level
\textsc{Stratified\-Group\-KFold}(5) with per-recording DASS labels
and three seeds, the same FMs land in the 0.45--0.58 band (Table~\ref{tab:master}
rows ``Frozen LP'' and ``Fine-tuned'' columns Stress).

Figure~\ref{fig:cvgap} measures the CV-protocol inflation directly on
our data: holding the FM, the recipe and the label constant, we switch
only the partition rule. Under per-rec DASS the gap is model-dependent
and much smaller than the $\sim$21\,pp uniform drop reported under
subject-DASS OR-aggregation (F07): LaBraM $+15.2$\,pp, REVE $+6.5$\,pp,
CBraMod $+1.2$\,pp. The honest-label mean gap is $+7.6$\,pp, roughly a
third of what the legacy trait-aggregated evaluation suggests. Every
subsequent number in this paper is anchored to the subject-level,
per-rec-DASS side of this comparison.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/main/fig2_cv_gap.pdf}
  \caption{%
    Trial-level vs.\ subject-level CV on the three FMs, same 70-recording
    Stress dataset, per-rec DASS labels, three seeds. Error bars are
    sample std. The protocol-driven gap is real but small and
    model-dependent, roughly one third of what the legacy subject-DASS
    setting suggests (F01).%
  }
  \label{fig:cvgap}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Single-seed numbers are not reproducible (F08)}

% EVIDENCE
On 70 recordings with 14 positive samples, microscopic initialisation
differences cascade through five CV folds and produce 10--20\,pp
balanced-accuracy swings even with \texttt{cudnn.deterministic=True}
and \texttt{benchmark=False}. The canonical LaBraM FT recipe on Stress
gives $0.443 \pm 0.083$ across seeds $\{42, 123, 2024\}$---a
99-percentile range that spans roughly 14\,pp. Two of our 10
permutation-null seeds ($0.607$ and $0.643$) beat \emph{all} real-label
seeds. We therefore treat any single-seed Stress number in the
published literature as an artefact of random initialisation, and in
this paper we require every Stress claim to be the mean of at least
three seeds; the frozen-LP ceiling additionally uses eight seeds.

\subsubsection{Classical features collapse under honest labels (F16, F03)}

% EVIDENCE
Under per-recording DASS on the same 70 recordings used by all FMs,
all classical methods we tried---Random Forest, Logistic Regression,
XGBoost, SVM with band-power and Hjorth features---fall to or below
chance (0.43--0.49 BA, all $\kappa \le 0$), Figure~\ref{fig:classical}.
The previously reported 0.666 RF number~\cite{komarov2020stress} was
obtained under subject-DASS OR-aggregation and is entirely an
aggregation artefact (F16).

This reverses the folk assumption that foundation models are
unnecessary for resting-state stress classification: on honest labels
the best FM frozen linear probe (LaBraM 0.605) is the \emph{only}
model family consistently above chance. The absolute number is modest,
but the gap to the best classical learner (0.49) is $\sim$11\,pp.

\subsection{Pillar B: Representation Diagnosis}
\label{sec:results:representation}

Having neutralised the three methodology traps, we now ask what the
frozen representations actually encode, separating subject identity
from label signal. This is where the FM-vs-classical gap is
mechanistically explained.

\subsubsection{Subject identity dominates frozen representations (F02, F13)}

% EVIDENCE + FIGURE
Representational similarity analysis on the frozen embeddings of all
twelve \{FM, dataset\} cells shows that the subject-identity
similarity exceeds the label similarity in \emph{all 12}
combinations (Figure~\ref{fig:fitness}a). Mixed-effects ICC on EEGMAT,
the strongest-biomarker dataset in our panel, attributes 71\,\% of
embedding variance to the subject random effect and only 5\,\% to the
label fixed effect. The same pattern holds across architectures:
LaBraM, CBraMod, and REVE all produce embedding geometries in which
``which subject this recording came from'' is a much stronger axis
than ``which label this recording carries'' (Figure~\ref{fig:fitness}b,
t-SNE by subject vs.\ by label).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{figures/main/fig3a_fitness_heatmap.pdf}\hfill
  \includegraphics[width=0.48\linewidth]{figures/main/fig3b_tsne_cross_dataset.png}
  \caption{%
    (a) RSA subject$/$label similarity ratio across 12 \{FM, dataset\}
    cells; every cell satisfies $r_{\text{subj}} > r_{\text{label}}$.
    (b) t-SNE of frozen LaBraM embeddings on a representative
    cross-dataset pool---clustering is by subject, not by label (F02).%
  }
  \label{fig:fitness}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{A linearly-separable label sub-space remains (F03)}

Despite the subject-identity dominance, the frozen FM representation
retains a low-SNR but linearly accessible label direction that the
classical band-power feature space does not. This is the
mechanistic explanation of Figure~\ref{fig:classical}: FMs learn a
subject fingerprint \emph{plus} a label sub-space, and the linear
probe finds the latter; hand-crafted band-power features lie in a
sub-space that overlaps the former but not the latter.

\subsubsection{Alpha lateralisation is the classical signal (F10)}

Random-forest feature importances on the classical pipeline show
13 of the top~20 features are alpha-band, concentrated in the right
hemisphere (T4, C4, CP4, P4) and right-frontal (Fp2, F8) electrodes.
This alpha-lateralisation signature matches the classical
stress/affect literature, and it sets the baseline neuroscience
prediction that \S\ref{sec:results:interp} tests against the three FMs.

\subsection{Pillar C: Cross-Dataset $\times$ Cross-Model FT Taxonomy}
\label{sec:results:taxonomy}

% THIS IS THE HEADLINE SECTION.
This section is the paper's central contribution. Holding the FM
constant and varying the dataset reveals that fine-tuning does not
have a single ``direction''---it has at least three, and which one you
get depends on a model~$\times$~dataset interaction that no existing
FM benchmark stratifies on.

\subsubsection{Representation-level pp $\Delta$ is dataset-dependent \emph{and} model-dependent (F17)}

We ran 3 models~$\times$~2 external datasets (ADFTD, TDBRAIN)
$\times$~3 seeds of matched fine-tuning and recomputed the pooled
label fraction ($\eta^2$) on the post-FT embeddings. The resulting
pp\,$\Delta$ matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:taxonomy}) shows three distinct
modes: \emph{injection} on ADFTD (LaBraM~$+5.2$\,pp, REVE~$+5.6$\,pp,
CBraMod nearly zero), \emph{erosion} on TDBRAIN (LaBraM~$-1.5$\,pp,
CBraMod~$-7.6$\,pp, REVE~$-5.6$\,pp, all on the opposite side of the
permutation null from zero), and a model-specific flip between the
two datasets. CBraMod, in particular, moves in the opposite direction
to the other two FMs on ADFTD---the model~$\times$~dataset
interaction is not a noise artefact.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/main/fig5_cross_dataset_taxonomy.pdf}
  \caption{%
    FT~$-$~Frozen pooled label fraction (pp\,$\Delta$) for
    3~FMs~$\times$~2 datasets, three seeds; error bars are
    cluster-bootstrap 95\,\% CIs resampled by subject. ADFTD injects
    label signal for LaBraM and REVE and leaves CBraMod flat; TDBRAIN
    erodes label signal for all three, with CBraMod losing the most

  }
  \label{fig:taxonomy}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Behavioural-level master table}

Table~\ref{tab:master} lifts the representation-level finding to the
downstream classifier. All twelve \{FM, dataset\} cells are now
populated at three seeds. Two behaviours immediately stand out.
First, CBraMod's frozen LP on EEGMAT is the highest cell in the entire
table ($0.731 \pm 0.053$), yet its fine-tuned counterpart
\emph{degrades} to $0.620 \pm 0.058$ ($-11.1$\,pp, the single largest
erosion in the table). The pretrained criss-cross attention has
already found the rest-vs-task axis, and full-parameter FT actively
destroys it. Second, on Stress every model shows a \emph{surface}
injection or erosion of $\pm 8$--$9$\,pp that looks strong in
isolation but fails to clear the permutation null
(\S\ref{sec:power_floor}).

\subsubsection{$N$-invariance of the taxonomy (F04)}

A natural concern is that the three modes are an artefact of the
different dataset sizes. We address this with matched subsampling: at
each sample-size rung $N \in \{17, 34, 70\}$ we draw 100 random
subsets, redo the full FT and variance-decomposition pipeline, and
recompute the pp\,$\Delta$ (Figure~\ref{fig:matched}). The injection
on ADFTD is $+5.22$\,pp at $N=17$---numerically indistinguishable from
its full-dataset value---and TDBRAIN's erosion remains $\approx -1$\,pp
at the same matched $N$. The taxonomy is therefore a property of the
\emph{label biology}, not of sample size. Because the matched-$N$
pipeline is computationally heavy we restrict it to LaBraM; we note
this as a limitation in \S\ref{sec:discussion}.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering

  \caption{%
    Matched-subsample pp\,$\Delta$ curves for LaBraM on the four
    datasets. At every sample-size rung $N$, the injection/erosion
    direction and approximate magnitude are preserved---the taxonomy
    is $N$-invariant (F04).%
  }
  \label{fig:matched}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Within-subject design is not a universal rescue (F09 + F14)}

If subject identity dominates the representation, one might hope that
a within-subject paired design would sidestep the problem entirely.
We test this with two within-subject benchmarks:

\begin{itemize}
  \item On \textbf{EEGMAT} (paired rest vs.\ arithmetic), LaBraM FT
    reaches $0.731 \pm 0.021$ BA and the frozen LP already reaches
    $0.671 \pm 0.056$ (Table~\ref{tab:master}). Within-subject works
    here.
  \item On \textbf{Stress} (leave-one-subject-out within-subject DSS
    trajectory classification, F14), all three FMs across three
    classifiers (centroid, 1-NN, logistic) fall to chance, with
    negative Cohen's~$\kappa$ (Supplementary Figure~S4).
\end{itemize}

The decisive variable is not ``within- vs.\ between-subject'' but the
\emph{strength of the within-subject contrast}. EEGMAT has a strong
paradigm-driven rest/task switch; Stress DSS is a low-SNR longitudinal
drift whose same-day EEG correlates are weak. The comparison serves as
the fifth line of evidence in \S\ref{sec:power_floor}.

\subsection{Neuroscience Interpretability}
\label{sec:results:interp}

For a TNSRE audience the central interpretability question is:
\emph{do the FMs' decisions correspond to a known neural signature,
or to an incidental statistical feature?} We attack this with three
complementary probes (spatial, spectral-correlational,
spectral-causal) and use their cross-model agreement as a signal-vs-noise
diagnostic: a real neural signature should appear consistently across
three different architectures; an architecture-specific pattern is
more likely an artefact.

\subsubsection{Spatial signatures (topomap)}

Gradient-based per-channel importance on the three FMs
(Figure~\ref{fig:interp}a) converges on right-hemisphere and
right-frontal electrodes, matching the classical
alpha-asymmetry prediction of \S\ref{sec:results:representation}
(F10). The cross-model convergence argues that the topographic
finding is a real neural signal rather than a per-model artefact.

\subsubsection{Spectral correlational (band RSA)}

Per-band representational similarity analysis
(Figure~\ref{fig:interp}b) shows that alpha and beta bands carry the
strongest similarity to the full-signal representation across all
three FMs on EEGMAT. Theta is dominant only on Stress and only for
CBraMod, an architecture-specific pattern we flag below.

\subsubsection{Spectral causal (band-stop ablation) and cross-model convergence}

We band-stop-filter the input in one of four bands (delta, theta,
alpha, beta), re-run the frozen linear probe, and measure the BA
drop. On EEGMAT (strong biomarker), LaBraM and REVE both peak at
alpha, and CBraMod's delta peak moves closer to alpha---a
cross-model convergence onto the classical cognitive-load signature
(Figure~\ref{fig:interp}c). On Stress, the three models diverge:
LaBraM peaks at beta, REVE at alpha, CBraMod always at delta. The
divergence is itself evidence that the ``stress'' signal each model
latches on to is architecture-specific rather than neurally grounded,
and is the fourth line of the power-floor argument below.

\subsection{Stress as a Statistical Power Floor}
\label{sec:power_floor}

This final subsection reframes every Stress-specific surface effect in
the paper. Five independent lines of evidence converge on the
conclusion that 70 recordings with 14 positives is below the
statistical power floor of our benchmark suite: not because the FMs
fail, but because the data cannot tell us whether they fail.

\paragraph{Surface effect sizes look convincing.}
Figure~\ref{fig:powerfloor}a reports the F05 three-seed best-HP
numbers: LaBraM $-8.1$\,pp erosion, CBraMod $+9.6$\,pp and REVE
$+8.3$\,pp injection, all with non-overlapping bootstrap 95\,\%~CIs
(Figure~\ref{fig:powerfloor}b) and Cohen's~$d$ above~2.5. At
face value these are publication-grade effects.

\paragraph{Permutation null says otherwise (F06, F19).}
LaBraM's canonical FT mean ($0.443 \pm 0.083$) is
\emph{indistinguishable} from the label-shuffled null
($0.497 \pm 0.086$, 10 perms, one-sided $p = 0.70$).
CBraMod and REVE's best-HP FT both sit at $p \approx 0.10$ (the 10-perm
floor), and two of the 10 LaBraM null seeds beat every real-label seed
(Supplementary Figure~S2). The bootstrap CIs are tight; the null
distribution is wide; the real-label signal is not separable from the
null at $N=70$.

\paragraph{Architecture confers no advantage (F20).}
Non-FM baselines trained from scratch on the same 70 recordings
(Figure~\ref{fig:powerfloor}c) reach $0.557 \pm 0.031$ BA for
ShallowConvNet and $0.518 \pm 0.097$ for EEGNet, overlapping every
FM best-HP FT cell. A 2017-vintage convolutional architecture matches
the 2024-vintage transformer FMs on this task---the ceiling is not
set by the encoder's capacity but by the labels.

\paragraph{Cross-model band divergence (\S\ref{sec:results:interp}).}
On Stress the three FMs peak at three different bands in the
band-stop ablation. A genuine neural signature should be
architecture-agnostic; its absence here is the fourth line of evidence.

\paragraph{Within-subject framing also fails (F14).}
As \S\ref{sec:results:taxonomy} documented, leave-one-subject-out DSS
trajectory classification lands at chance for all three FMs across
three classifier families, even though the same within-subject
framing succeeds on EEGMAT. The failure is dataset-intrinsic, not a
methodology limitation (fifth line of evidence).

\paragraph{Take-away.}
On 70 recordings with 14 positive samples, \emph{no} FM-related
scientific claim on UCSD Stress can be distinguished from label
noise. This is a benchmark-design caveat rather than an FM failure,
and it reproduces and extends the cross-subject-affect failure
patterns reported independently by three recent benchmark
papers~\cite{eegfmbench2025,adabrain2025,brain4fms2026} (F11). We
recommend $N > 200$ subjects with balanced class prevalence as a
minimum for any future resting-state stress FM benchmark, and we use
Stress in the rest of this paper as the reference case of what
undersized benchmarks do to FM comparison claims, not as a model
ranking.
