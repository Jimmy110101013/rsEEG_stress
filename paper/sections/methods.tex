\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:methods:datasets}

We evaluate on three resting-state EEG datasets spanning a range of clinical
signal strengths (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Datasets evaluated. Signal strength is qualitative.}
\label{tab:datasets}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrlll}
\toprule
Dataset & Subjects & Recordings & Protocol & Labels & Signal \\
\midrule
UCSD Stress (ours) & 17 & 70 & 5\,min EO, longitudinal & DASS (binary) & Weak \\
ADFTD~\citep{adftd} & 65 & 195 & $\sim$14\,min EC & AD vs HC & Strong \\
TDBRAIN~\citep{tdbrain} & 359 & 734 & EO+EC, multi-session & MDD vs HC & Moderate \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{UCSD Stress.} 17 subjects, 70 valid recordings after a 400\,s
duration filter, 5\,min eyes-open resting protocol, multiple visits per
subject. Labels are derived from DASS (depression-anxiety-stress) scale: a
recording is labelled \emph{increase} if the subject's longitudinal DASS
trajectory crosses a clinical threshold; \emph{normal} otherwise. We use
the \texttt{subject-dass} label mode in which all recordings from a
subject classified as \emph{increase} share the positive label, ensuring
subject-level CV is well-defined.

\paragraph{ADFTD~\citep{adftd}.} OpenNeuro ds004504. 65 subjects (36 AD,
29 HC), 19-channel resting-state EEG. We split each $\sim$14\,min recording
into three contiguous $\sim$4.5\,min pseudo-recordings (\texttt{n\_splits=3})
to enable within-subject variance analysis, yielding 195 ``recordings'' for
the variance decomposition.

\paragraph{TDBRAIN~\citep{tdbrain}.} 359 subjects (94 HC, 640 MDD-positive
recordings across multi-session subjects), 19 channels, 500\,Hz, both
eyes-open and eyes-closed conditions used as separate recordings. Subject
identity is preserved across conditions and sessions for subject-level CV.

\subsection{Foundation Models}
\label{sec:methods:fms}

We evaluate three FMs spanning distinct architectural families:
\textbf{LaBraM}~\citep{labram2024} (ViT with channel position embeddings,
embed dim 200), \textbf{REVE}~\citep{reve2024} (ViT with 4D Fourier
positional encoding and MAE pretraining, embed dim 512), and
\textbf{CBraMod}~\citep{cbramod2025} (criss-cross transformer with
asymmetric channel position encoding, embed dim 200).

For frozen feature extraction, all three are restricted to the 19-channel
common 10-20 montage shared across our datasets, with appropriate channel
mapping (10-10 to 10-20 aliases applied where necessary).

\subsection{Cross-Validation Protocols}
\label{sec:methods:cv}

We compare two protocols on identical data:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=0.25em,itemsep=0.1em,leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Trial-level CV}: stratified 5-fold over windows, with
    multiple windows from a single subject potentially appearing in both
    train and test folds. This is the protocol behind much of the
    published EEG stress literature.
  \item \textbf{Subject-level CV}: stratified group 5-fold with subject as
    the group, ensuring no subject appears in both train and test. This
    is our primary metric.
\end{itemize}

For both, we report balanced accuracy (\BA{}) at the recording level
(majority vote across windows of a recording), as well as per-window
metrics for diagnostic purposes.

\subsection{Variance Decomposition via $\eta^{2}$}
\label{sec:methods:eta}

Given a feature matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ with subject labels
$s$ and task labels $y$, we compute $\eta^{2}$ for each grouping factor
via one-way ANOVA per feature dimension and average across dimensions:
\begin{equation}
  \eta^{2}_{\mathrm{factor}} =
    \frac{1}{d} \sum_{j=1}^{d}
    \frac{\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{between}}^{(j)}}
         {\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{total}}^{(j)}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{between}}^{(j)}$ is the between-group sum of
squares for feature $j$ under the chosen factor (subject or task). The
ratio $\eta^{2}_{\mathrm{subject}} / \eta^{2}_{\mathrm{label}}$ quantifies
the relative dominance of subject identity over the diagnostic signal.

To enable fair comparison across datasets with different subject counts, we
also report a subject-count-matched estimate: for ADFTD ($N{=}65$) we
randomly subsample $N{=}17$ subjects 10 times and report the mean and
standard deviation.
