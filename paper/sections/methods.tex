\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

% --- Overview -----------------------------------------------------
Given resting-state scalp EEG recordings with a per-recording stress
label derived from the DASS-21 questionnaire, our goal is to
(i)~benchmark three publicly available EEG foundation models (FMs) on a
stress-classification task, (ii)~isolate \emph{what those FMs actually
encode} from what their downstream balanced accuracy (BA) suggests, and
(iii)~characterise the fine-tuning (FT) effect across four datasets and
three architectures. The pipeline in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}
emphasises three design choices that, while individually small, each
silently invalidate the comparison if handled carelessly: a leak-free
cross-validation (CV) protocol, per-model input scaling, and
multi-seed aggregation. Section~\ref{sec:method:data} defines the
dataset and labels; Section~\ref{sec:method:cv} specifies the CV
protocol; Section~\ref{sec:method:norm} details per-model input
normalisation and window length; Section~\ref{sec:method:seed}
describes multi-seed aggregation and determinism;
Section~\ref{sec:method:null} introduces the permutation null used to
gauge effect size against chance; Section~\ref{sec:method:variance}
lists the variance-decomposition measures used to diagnose the learnt
representations; Section~\ref{sec:method:report} states how statistics
are reported.

% --- 2.1 Data and labels -----------------------------------------
\subsection{Datasets and Labels}
\label{sec:method:data}

% MOTIVATION: what labels are, why per-rec matters
The Stress dataset from Komarov, Ko, and Jung~\cite{komarov2020stress} is a
longitudinal resting-state study of Taiwanese graduate students: every
recording is accompanied by a same-day DASS-21 self-report (Depression,
Anxiety, Stress subscales) and a Daily Stress Score (DSS). Any
within-subject aggregation of the label---for example, assigning each
subject a single ``trait'' label by OR-pooling their recording-level
DASS---silently converts a state-classification task into a trait
classifier and inflates balanced accuracy on both trial-level and
subject-level CV by \emph{trait memorisation} (we document this in
Finding F07). We therefore use the state label at its native
granularity.

% DESIGN --- Stress
Of the 92 recordings (17 subjects) released with the original Stress
dataset, we retain the 70 recordings that carry both DASS-21 and DSS
labels so that downstream within-subject DSS analyses are possible on
the same subset; the final label is \emph{per-recording DASS increase
versus normal} (\texttt{--label dass} in the released code), yielding
14 positive and 56 negative recordings across 17 subjects. Acquisition
uses a 30-channel 10--20 montage at 500\,Hz with eyes-closed
resting-state paradigm, 5--10\,min per session. The design is
\emph{within-subject longitudinal, between-subject} for the state
label: each participant contributes 4--7 sessions spanning weeks to
months, and the binary DASS label varies across a participant's own
recordings so the task is strictly state classification rather than
trait identification.

% DESIGN --- cross-dataset benchmark
Three external public EEG datasets provide an out-of-domain benchmark
of the same three FMs, chosen to span the axis from strong to weak
label biology:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{ADFTD} (Miltiadous et al., 2023~\cite{adftd}):
    routine-clinic scalp EEG from 88 participants with Alzheimer's
    disease, frontotemporal dementia, or healthy controls; we use the
    AD-vs-HC subset (195 recordings, 65 subjects), acquired at
    500\,Hz on a 19-channel 10--20 montage with eyes-closed
    resting-state paradigm of $\sim$15\,min. AD is known to produce a
    robust slow-wave (theta/delta) increase in resting EEG, making
    this a \emph{strong-biomarker, between-subject} benchmark.
  \item \textbf{TDBRAIN} (van Dijk et al., 2022~\cite{tdbrain}): the
    Two Decades Brainclinics Research Archive, a multi-disorder
    clinical EEG repository; we use the major-depressive-disorder
    (MDD) vs healthy-control subset (734 recordings, 359 subjects)
    acquired at 500\,Hz on a 26-channel 10--20 montage, eyes-closed
    resting-state, $\sim$2\,min per recording, reduced to 19 common
    channels with the other datasets. MDD produces only a
    \emph{weak and contested} resting-EEG biomarker in the literature
    (small alpha-asymmetry and frontal-midline-theta effects), making
    this a \emph{weak-biomarker, between-subject, high-$N$} benchmark.
  \item \textbf{EEGMAT} (Zyma et al., 2019~\cite{eegmat}, PhysioNet):
    36 healthy university students performing serial-subtraction
    mental arithmetic; each subject provides a 180-second eyes-closed
    rest recording and a 60-second arithmetic-task recording, yielding
    72 recordings paired \emph{within-subject by design}. Acquisition
    uses a 19-channel 10--20 montage at 500\,Hz. Task-evoked
    cognitive-load contrasts are a classical and well-studied EEG
    biomarker (alpha suppression, theta increase), making this a
    \emph{strong-biomarker, within-subject, paired-contrast}
    benchmark---the cleanest available control for subject identity
    in our panel.
\end{itemize}

All four datasets are resampled to 200\,Hz, notch-filtered at line
frequency, bandpass-filtered 0.5--45\,Hz, and re-referenced as per each
FM's canonical preprocessing recipe. Channels are aligned to the
common 19-electrode 10--20 subset for the three external datasets; the
Stress dataset is retained at its full 30-channel montage because all
three FMs accept arbitrary channel count.

% ADVANTAGE
The four datasets deliberately span the biomarker-strength $\times$
design-axis grid: strong-biomarker between-subject (ADFTD),
weak-biomarker between-subject (TDBRAIN), strong-biomarker
within-subject paired (EEGMAT), and weak-biomarker within-subject
longitudinal (Stress). This grid is what lets the cross-dataset
taxonomy in Section~\ref{sec:results} attribute fine-tuning-direction
differences to \emph{label biology} rather than to sample size or
dataset idiosyncrasies. Holding the Stress label at the recording
level and matching the data subset across frozen-LP, FT, and
within-subject DSS analyses additionally removes the two well-known
confounders (label aggregation and sample mismatch) that would
otherwise make it impossible to attribute a balanced-accuracy change
to any one design choice.

% --- 2.2 CV protocol ---------------------------------------------
\subsection{Cross-Validation Protocol}
\label{sec:method:cv}

% MOTIVATION
In EEG resting-state datasets that contain multiple recordings per
subject, \emph{trial-level} random splits place recordings from the
same subject on both sides of the train/test boundary, which the model
can exploit via subject-identity features. Roy et al.~\cite{roy2019review}
document this leakage across the deep-learning-for-EEG literature.
Prior foundation-model reports on the UCSD Stress dataset under
trial-level CV reach 0.90 balanced accuracy~\cite{wang2025stress}; our
subject-level CV measurements pull the same models to
0.45--0.60~(Table~\ref{tab:master}), confirming that the majority of
the reported gain is CV-protocol-driven rather than model-driven
(F01).

% DESIGN
We adopt \textsc{Stratified\-Group\-KFold} ($k{=}5$), grouped by
\texttt{patient\_id}, as the primary evaluation protocol; every test
fold contains only subjects unseen during training and validation.
Trial-level \textsc{Stratified\-KFold} ($k{=}5$) is reported in a
single figure (Figure~\ref{fig:cvgap}) solely for reference-paper
comparison and is labelled ``subject leakage present'' in every caption
and legend. Predictions are pooled across test folds before computing
balanced accuracy, Cohen's~$\kappa$, macro-F1 and a subject-level
confusion matrix.

% ADVANTAGE
Subject-level grouping is the minimum sufficient control against
trait-memorisation artefacts in EEG state-classification tasks with
per-subject repeated recordings; it makes every reported effect size in
this paper a lower bound on the model's trial-level number under the
same recipe.

% --- 2.3 Per-model input normalisation ---------------------------
\subsection{Per-Model Input Normalisation and Window Length}
\label{sec:method:norm}

% MOTIVATION
Each FM was pre-trained with a specific input scale, and any scaling
drift at fine-tuning time interacts destructively with the pretrained
weights. A single global \texttt{--norm} flag applied uniformly across
a multi-model sweep will silently destroy two of the three models we
benchmark---an issue we encountered early in development and that we
flag explicitly here because it is not documented in the original FM
releases.

% DESIGN
We apply model-specific input normalisation and window length:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{LaBraM}~\cite{labram2024}: per-channel z-score; 5\,s
    windows at 200\,Hz.
  \item \textbf{CBraMod}~\cite{cbramod2025}: \emph{no} external
    normalisation---the extractor internally divides by 100; z-scoring
    the input on top drives it to near-zero, collapsing training
    within a single epoch. 5\,s windows at 200\,Hz.
  \item \textbf{REVE}~\cite{reve2025}: \emph{no} external
    normalisation---its patch-embedding layer was trained on
    $\mu$V-scale data and is scale-sensitive. 10\,s windows at 200\,Hz
    (the native patch size).
\end{itemize}
Classical non-FM baselines (EEGNet, ShallowConvNet, DeepConvNet,
EEGConformer) use per-channel z-score in line with their original
publications. All frozen-LP vs.\ FT comparisons are window-matched to
their own model; a 5\,s~vs.\ 10\,s sensitivity check for REVE is
reported in the supplementary material.

% ADVANTAGE
Per-model normalisation preserves the distributional assumptions of
each pretrained encoder, letting the comparison in
Table~\ref{tab:master} reflect architectural differences rather than
undocumented input-scaling incompatibilities.

% --- 2.4 Determinism and multi-seed ------------------------------
\subsection{Determinism and Multi-Seed Aggregation}
\label{sec:method:seed}

% MOTIVATION
In the 70-recording / 14-positive regime, single-seed balanced accuracy
can swing by more than 10\,pp between otherwise-identical runs even
with cuDNN determinism enabled. Any claim anchored to a single seed is
therefore an artefact of random initialisation, not of the method.

% DESIGN
We set
\texttt{torch.backends.cudnn.deterministic=True},
\texttt{benchmark=False}, and a fixed Python/NumPy/PyTorch seed before
dataset construction. Every Stress finding in the paper is the mean of
three seeds $\{42, 123, 2024\}$, and the frozen-LP ceiling on Stress
additionally uses eight seeds (F05). Standard deviations use the sample
convention ($n{-}1$ divisor); we provide an audit script
(\texttt{scripts/audit\_std\_convention.py}) that flags any cell in the
paper deviating from this convention.

% ADVANTAGE
Three-seed aggregation, combined with cuDNN determinism, converts
``weekend-run-to-weekend-run'' irreproducibility into a reported
standard deviation that a reviewer can sanity-check; it also exposes
power-floor effects (Section~\ref{sec:method:null}) that a single-seed
run would hide.

% --- 2.5 Permutation null ----------------------------------------
\subsection{Permutation-Null Protocol}
\label{sec:method:null}

% MOTIVATION
In small-$N$ EEG benchmarks, a balanced accuracy of 0.60 on a
stratified split can be statistically indistinguishable from chance.
Cohen's~$\kappa$ and bootstrap CIs each address a different aspect of
this, but neither rules out that the specific label assignment interacts
with subject-level imbalance to mimic a real effect. A permutation null
that shuffles labels at the recording level and re-runs the full
pipeline directly quantifies this.

% DESIGN
\texttt{train\_ft.py --permute-labels <seed>} shuffles recording-level
labels before any CV split and then runs the complete FT pipeline
(same hyperparameters, same early stopping, same seed-setting
discipline). We repeat this for 10 null seeds per
\{model, dataset\} cell on Stress, giving a null distribution of size
$3~\text{FMs} \times 10 = 30$. The $p$-value is the fraction of null
runs whose balanced accuracy exceeds the real-label mean.

% ADVANTAGE
A permutation null is label-free by construction, so any residual
signal in the null distribution is attributable to CV-protocol
imbalance rather than to the label. This is the single cleanest
diagnostic for a small-$N$ benchmark like Stress, and it is the
measurement that downgrades every ``surface'' FT injection claim in
Section~\ref{sec:results} to a power-floor caveat.

% --- 2.6 Variance decomposition ----------------------------------
\subsection{Representation-Level Variance Decomposition}
\label{sec:method:variance}

% MOTIVATION
Downstream balanced accuracy alone cannot tell whether an FM learnt
subject-invariant label structure or whether its classifier head
compensates for a representation that mostly encodes subject identity.
To separate these two regimes we measure how much of the embedding
variance is attributable to label versus subject.

% DESIGN
For every \{model, dataset\} cell we extract the frozen pooled
embedding of each recording and compute:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Pooled label fraction} $\eta^2$: the fraction of
    embedding variance explained by the label after pooling over CV
    folds; for FT runs this is recomputed on the post-FT embeddings
    and reported as a paired $\Delta$ (pp).
  \item \textbf{RSA}: representational similarity analysis of the
    embedding distance matrix against (a)~a subject-identity indicator
    matrix and (b)~a label-identity matrix; we report the ratio
    \(r_{\text{subj}}/r_{\text{label}}\).
  \item \textbf{Mixed-effects ICC}: variance partitioning into a
    subject random effect, a label fixed effect, and residual noise.
  \item \textbf{Matched subsampling}: at each sample-size rung
    $N \in \{17, 34, 70, 140, \ldots\}$ we draw 100 subsamples and
    recompute the above measures to verify that the effect is not an
    $N$ artefact.
  \item \textbf{Cluster bootstrap}: 1{,}000 subject-resampled
    bootstrap replicates yield 95\%~CIs for every FT~$-$~Frozen
    difference.
\end{itemize}
All intermediate JSONs are shipped with the code release under
\texttt{results/studies/exp\{04,09,13\}/}.

% ADVANTAGE
This panel of measures gives a single representation-level verdict per
cell---``label injection'', ``label erosion'' or ``null''---that is
robust to the idiosyncrasies of any one classifier head and is the
direct input to the cross-dataset taxonomy in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

% --- 2.7 Statistical reporting -----------------------------------
\subsection{Statistical Reporting}
\label{sec:method:report}

All mean~$\pm$~std values use the sample convention ($n{-}1$ divisor);
bootstrap CIs are reported as $[\text{low}, \text{high}]$; effect sizes
between paired frozen-vs-FT runs use Cohen's~$d$. Balanced accuracy is
always computed on pooled global predictions after all folds complete,
never averaged fold-means, so our reported numbers are directly
comparable to the literature that evaluates on aggregate predictions.

% --- 2.8 Implementation details ---------------------------------
\subsection{Implementation Details}
\label{sec:method:impl}

All models are trained with AdamW ($\beta_1{=}0.9$, $\beta_2{=}0.999$,
weight decay $0.05$) for up to 50 epochs with 3-epoch linear warm-up
followed by a plateau-reactive scheduler, early stopping with patience
15 on the validation SMA-3 balanced accuracy, gradient clipping at
2.0 and a batch size of 32 windows. The minority class is augmented
with 75\% window overlap (\texttt{--aug-overlap 0.75}). Fine-tuning
uses per-model best-HP learning rates from a $4\times3$
grid sweep (F05): LaBraM $1\times10^{-4}$ with encoder LR scale $1.0$;
CBraMod $1\times10^{-5}$ with encoder LR scale $0.1$; REVE
$3\times10^{-5}$ with encoder LR scale $0.1$. Linear probes use the
same optimiser and scheduler on the classifier head only, with the
encoder held frozen throughout. All runs are executed on a single
NVIDIA A100~40G under PyTorch~2.5.1~+~CUDA~12.4 inside the
\texttt{stress} conda environment documented in the code release.
