\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Foundation models (FMs) pretrained on large unlabelled EEG corpora have
recently reported strong performance on downstream clinical tasks
\citep{labram2024,cbramod2025,reve2025,kauffmann2025critique}. A
representative claim comes from Wang et al.\ \citep{wang2025stress},
who report $0.9047$ balanced accuracy (BA) on the Komarov--Ko--Jung
resting-state stress dataset \citep{komarov2020stress} ---
17 subjects, 92 recordings, per-recording DASS-21 self-reports.
Independent EEG FM
benchmarks~\citep{brain4fms2026,eegfmbench2025,adabrain2025,eegbench2025},
meanwhile, converge on the empirical observation that FMs do not
consistently outperform simpler models on cross-subject clinical
tasks. We ask \emph{when} an EEG FM claim on a small clinical cohort
should be expected to hold. Our contribution is not a new
architecture, a new loss, or a new dataset, but a \emph{diagnosis}:
frozen FM representations overwhelmingly encode subject identity;
under subject-respecting evaluation, the Wang headline is
attenuated to a $0.52$--$0.58$ band shared by seven architectures
spanning five orders of magnitude in parameter count; and a
controlled paired comparison isolates the operative variable as the
within-subject neural contrast strength of the target label, not
model capacity.

We call this pattern \textbf{Subject-Dominance Limits (SDL)}: when the
downstream label corresponds to a within-subject neural contrast that
is strong relative to subject-level representational variance, FM
fine-tuning projects the label variance onto an otherwise
subject-dominated representation and delivers measurable rescue.
When the neural contrast is weak or absent, no architecture available
to us escapes the subject-dominance ceiling. SDL supplies the
mechanistic layer under the aggregate benchmark observation.

If the failure locus is contrast strength rather than model capacity,
the natural next step is to ask \emph{how to decide, before running a
benchmark, whether a target clinical task will admit FM rescue}. We
propose a \emph{pre-benchmark diagnostic question}: \emph{does the
label have a within-subject neural contrast in the published
neuroscience literature?} If no independent evidence exists for such
a contrast, reported subject-level benchmarks should be interpreted
as bounded by the subject-dominance ceiling --- regardless of how
large the pretrained model or how careful the fine-tuning recipe ---
and the reader should expect compact from-scratch baselines to match
or exceed the FM result.

Section~\ref{sec:results} presents four experimental chapters
delivering the SDL diagnosis --- subject-atlas quantification, the
Wang-protocol audit, the paired within-subject contrast experiment,
and the architecture-independence check --- followed by the
pre-benchmark contrast-anchoring protocol in
Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}.
