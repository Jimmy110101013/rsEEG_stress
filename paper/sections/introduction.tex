\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

EEG foundation models trained on large unlabelled corpora have been proposed
as a general-purpose feature extractor for clinical and affective EEG tasks.
Recent models including LaBraM~\citep{labram2024}, CBraMod~\citep{cbramod2025},
and REVE~\citep{reve2024} report strong downstream performance on a range of
benchmarks, raising hope that pretrained representations could enable
data-efficient diagnosis on small clinical cohorts where supervised
end-to-end training is infeasible.

Resting-state stress detection is a natural target. The clinical use case
--- pre-diagnosing prolonged stress from a short eyes-open recording --- is
high-impact, the labels (validated psychometric instruments such as DASS) are
trustworthy, and the data acquisition is cheap. Prior work using
trial-level cross-validation has reported balanced accuracies above 90\% on
this task~\citep{wang2025stress}, suggesting the problem is essentially
solved.

\paragraph{Our findings.}
We show that this optimistic picture is an artefact of evaluation
methodology, not of model capacity. Concretely:

\begin{enumerate}[topsep=0.25em,itemsep=0.1em,leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{The trial-vs-subject cross-validation gap is large and
    consistent.} All three foundation models we evaluate drop $\sim$21
    balanced-accuracy points when moving from trial-level to subject-level
    splits. The previously-reported 90\% headline numbers fail to generalise
    to unseen subjects.

  \item \textbf{Frozen FM features encode subject identity, not diagnostic
    signal.} Variance decomposition via $\eta^{2}$ shows subject identity
    explains 11--13$\times$ more feature variance than task labels --- a
    pattern that holds across three architecturally distinct FMs and two
    clinical datasets (stress and Alzheimer's).

  \item \textbf{Classical features match FM performance.} A random forest on
    156 hand-crafted band-power features achieves $0.666$ \BA{} on stress,
    statistically indistinguishable from fine-tuned LaBraM ($0.656$). The
    same parity holds on Alzheimer's ($0.753$ vs.\ $0.752$).

  \item \textbf{Standard mitigations fail.} Subject-adversarial training via
    gradient reversal degrades stress classification from $0.656$ to
    $0.537$. LEAD-style auxiliary subject losses behave similarly. The
    diagnostic signal is too weak to amplify by removing subject information
    alone.

  \item \textbf{The ceiling is method-independent and signal-limited.}
    Across foundation models, classical baselines, and adversarial
    variants, balanced accuracy on subject-level CV converges to
    $\sim$0.65 with $N{=}17$. We characterise this as the upper bound for
    binary cross-subject zero-shot prediction under our task framing.
\end{enumerate}

We argue this is not a refutation of resting-state EEG for stress, but a
characterisation of the wrong question. Subject identity dominance is a
\emph{useful} structural property if the question is reframed: ``has this
person's EEG drifted from \emph{their own} baseline?'' rather than ``does
this generic EEG segment come from a stressed person?''. Section~\ref{sec:discussion}
develops this implication and motivates personalised within-subject
modelling as the productive path forward.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}[topsep=0.25em,itemsep=0.1em,leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item First multi-FM, multi-dataset cross-subject evaluation for
    resting-state stress detection with rigorous subject-level CV.
  \item Quantitative diagnosis of subject-identity dominance via variance
    decomposition, validated across three FMs and three datasets (Stress,
    ADFTD, TDBRAIN).
  \item Negative results for two standard mitigation strategies
    (subject-adversarial and LEAD-style subject losses).
  \item Evidence that classical band-power features match FMs at this
    signal level, with no GPU and no pretraining.
  \item A reframing of the task that preserves the underlying clinical hope
    and points at a tractable next experiment.
\end{itemize}
